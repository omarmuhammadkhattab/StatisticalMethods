SECTION 01
What is Statistics?

Statistics is the science concerned with designing data collection methods, and various techniques for classifying, organizing, summarizing, displaying and analyzing these data in order to obtain statistical indicators and information necessary for making appropriate decisions.

💡

Think of statistics as a pipeline: you start with raw Data, then you classify, summarize, organize, display, and analyze it — and the output is useful Information to make decisions.

Population vs Sample

🌍 Population

The total group of elements under study. It can be limited (countable) or unlimited (cannot be fully counted). Example: All hospitals in Cairo.

🔬 Sample

A subset of the population. We use samples when it's impractical to study the entire population. Example: 500 families selected to estimate Egypt's average family income.

Types of Statistical Data

Hover over each node below to see its definition and examples.

📊 Statistical Data
🏷️ Qualitative Data
Qualitative (Categorical)Descriptive data that cannot have math operations (addition, subtraction) performed on it. It describes categories or qualities.
🔢 Quantitative Data
Quantitative (Numerical)Data that comes in the form of numbers. You can perform mathematical calculations on it and arrange it.
🏷️ Nominal
Nominal DataDescriptive categories with NO natural order. Examples: Blood type (A, B, O, AB), Gender (Male/Female), Nationality.
🔀 Ordinal
Ordinal DataDescriptive categories WITH a meaningful order/ranking. Examples: Satisfaction (Poor, Good, Excellent), Education level, Service quality.
🔵 Discrete
Discrete DataCountable, separate values. You can list each possible value. Examples: Number of patients, Number of accidents, Number of crimes.
〰️ Continuous
Continuous DataMeasured values that can take any value in a range. Examples: Age, Weight, Height, Income, Temperature.

Other Data Types

TIME SERIES
Time Series
Data collected at successive time periods. Used to discover patterns and forecast the future.
→ Daily surgeries over a year
CROSS-SECTION
Cross-Sectional
Data collected at a single point in time across multiple subjects or units.
→ Patients in all hospitals in October
PANEL
Panel / Longitudinal
A mix of time series AND cross-section. Multiple subjects tracked over multiple time periods.
→ 20 patients tracked for 10 weeks
BINARY
Binary
Data with only two values: 0 (failure) or 1 (success). Follows Bernoulli distribution.
→ Treated (1) / Not treated (0)
COUNT
Count Data
Non-negative integers from counting rather than measuring. Binomial or Poisson.
→ # patients cured out of 5
BIG DATA
Big vs Small Data
Big data exceeds traditional tools' capacity. Small data fits standard statistical methods.
→ IoT sensor logs vs. hospital survey

SECTION 04
Levels of Measurement

The level of measurement determines what statistical tests you can use. They go from least to most informative.

NOMINAL — Level 1
Nominal
The lowest level. Numbers are used only for identification — categories have no meaningful order or difference in magnitude.
Examples: Marital status, Blood type, Car type, Nationality
ORDINAL — Level 2
Ordinal
Has a logical order or preference between categories, but the differences between ranks are NOT equal or meaningful.
Examples: Education level, Consumer satisfaction, Service quality
INTERVAL — Level 3
Interval
Has meaningful differences between values, but NO true zero point. Zero is just an agreed reference, not absence of the quality.
Examples: Temperature (°C), Intelligence scores, Aggression scale
RATIO — Level 4
Ratio
The highest level. Has a true zero (zero = absence). Differences AND ratios are meaningful. You can say "twice as much."
Examples: Weight, Number of patients, Distance, Income

SECTION 06
Introduction to SPSS

SPSS stands for Statistical Package for Social Sciences. Despite its name, it is used across many branches of science. It is one of the best packages for statistical presentation and analysis in the world.

Main Windows in SPSS

WindowArabic NamePurpose
Data Viewنافذة تحرير البياناتDisplay, enter, and store data in rows (cases) and columns (variables)
Variable Viewنافذة تحرير المتغيراتDefine variables: type, label, values, missing, format, measurement level
Output Viewerنافذة عرض المخرجاتShows results of statistical analysis — saved separately from data file

Defining Variables in SPSS — 7 Steps

StepWhat You DoNotes
1. NameGive the variable a nameMust start with a letter. No spaces, no special chars (!?*). Max 8 chars in older versions.
2. TypeSet data typeNumeric (default), String (text), Date, Dollar, etc.
3. Width & DecimalsColumn width and decimal placesDefault: width=8, decimals=2
4. LabelFull description of the variablee.g. variable named "x1" → label is the full survey question
5. ValuesAssign codes to categoriese.g. Gender: 1 = Male, 2 = Female
6. MissingDefine missing value codesTell SPSS which values to treat as missing data
7. MeasureSet measurement levelNominal, Ordinal, or Scale (Ratio/Interval)
💡

Tip: Use numeric codes for categorical variables whenever possible (e.g., 1 = Male, 2 = Female). This allows SPSS to process them more efficiently than text strings.
